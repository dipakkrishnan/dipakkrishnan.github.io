`Deep Review` is a skill meant for thorough review of papers and technical artifacts created with significant AI assistance. The tool takes in a technical artifact and produces a report of findings that a user can utilize to fix structural and local errors in their work. I built it for two kinds of users: researchers generating papers with AI assistance and reviewers overwhelmed by the resulting surge in paper submissions. I tested the skill against a recent preprint that had errors the authors fixed and the tool flagged the critical errors without seeing the corrected revision. This post details the problem space and how the tool works.

### The verification gap in technical paper writing

Frontier AI models are reducing the time it takes for researchers to produce complex technical artifacts, but their outputs still require rigorous verification before they can be trusted. The problem is clear in domains that do not have a tightly coupled generation-verification loop.

Coding and formal math have strong verification loops bolstered by tests and proofs. In research, a paper's strength hinges on the novelty of its results and the correctness of its methods. Experts in their fields use their accumulated knowledge to quickly assess the quality of the contribution and the solidity of the paper's methods - but there is no ground truth verification that can be applied to a paper's structure without context.

Papers are increasingly generated with AI assistance. Researchers bring taste, judgement and domain knowledge while AI writes code, generates drafts and synthesizes existing literature. The end results *look* plausible but often contain two common classes of error:

- Structural errors
  - Inaccurate contribution framing
  - Applying a theorem incorrectly
  - Drawing a conclusion without requisite evidence
- Local errors
  - Off by one issues
  - Citation inconsistencies
  - Bugs in code samples
  - Missing proof steps

Structural errors compound. An inaccurate framing of prior work or a theorem applied incorrectly often defeats the central claim the entire paper is making. Local errors are hard to find - requiring careful passes to root them out. Both classes of error were easier to catch when humans drove the research loop on their own. Research used to be a serial process of hypothesis and experimentation that led to a crystal clear understanding of the work as a byproduct of execution. This is no longer the case and leads to two concrete problems:

- Researchers do not have a crisp mental model of the artifact, so the time and effort to review drastically increase
- The cost of writing a paper has dropped significantly, leading to an exploding number of paper submissions and overburdened reviewers
  - NeurIPS submissions more than doubled from [9,467 in 2020 to 21,575 in 2025](https://blog.neurips.cc/2025/09/30/reflections-on-the-2025-review-process-from-the-program-committee-chairs/), requiring more than 20,000 reviewers, and [AAAI-26 drew roughly 30,000 initial submissions](https://eu.36kr.com/en/p/3442011275122308) - about double the prior year.

### What's missing in the current review tool landscape

Users largely have three options to review their artifacts today: general AI chat interfaces, manual review, or utilizing coding agents.

Using Claude or ChatGPT to review can help, but the system isn't specialized to inspect the artifact over tens of minutes or hours, nor does it have full context on the reviewer's goals. Unstructured chat produces surface-level reviews that don't go deep enough to decompose structural errors and why they exist.

Manual review is clearly arduous and error-prone, even if it allows the user to exercise their expert judgement over the structural foundations of the paper.

Coding agents or knowledge work equivalents like Cowork can speed up generation and verification, especially on executable parts of the artifact. Coding agents are a strong medium for review. Fundamentally, coding agents are excellent at using computers. Writing and running code, managing context coherently and parallelizing work to multiple agents are all extremely useful capabilities to run a rigorous review (and would be useful for a human too!).

However, agents don't yet have the procedural context for how to conduct a grounded review, nor are they built to pressure-test key components of the artifact in context without explicit instructions. `Deep Review` is the procedural scaffolding that binds together the execution capability of the coding agent harness with context on how to conduct a rigorous review.

### How Deep Review works

Users can invoke `Deep Review` like an expert reviewer directly from a coding agent interface - like Cowork or Codex.

The user uploads an artifact file and asks the agent in chat to invoke the skill to review. To make this concrete, I threaded a real run through this section - a review of a publicly available May 2026 ML preprint, documented fully in the appendix. Invocation of the skill is fairly standard:

> `Use deep-review to review @testv1.pdf`

The agent runs a calibration interview with the user to get a sense of what the artifact is, who the audience is and what to spend review time on. For example, the agent will ask if it should look specifically at math / proof correctness only or focus on literature synthesis or positioning. It will ask the user if they are looking to submit the paper to a specific journal or conference - say AMIA or ICLR.

In the example run, the second calibration question asks the user where to focus:

> **Where do you most want scrutiny focused?**<br />
> ☑ Theorem 1 & proof - rigor of Lemma 1/Theorem 1, hidden assumptions, gap between full-rank W claim and proof<br />
> ☑ Theory-to-practice gap - whether the Gumbel-softmax hard-WTA experiments actually test the theorem's optimal-predictor assumptions<br />
> ☑ Empirical design - fairness of the MLPₓ vs MLPẑ comparison, metrics, seeds/statistics<br />
> ☐ Literature & novelty - positioning vs prior disentanglement work

Once the user answers the calibration interview, the system will checkpoint its learnings about the user, pull in useful information about the user and their background that it has accrued in previous review sessions, and begin its review flow. The model decomposes the review into specialist passes driven by subagents that attempt to find flaws in specific dimensions of the paper.

Subagents can write code to verify theorems and, if needed, run web searches to pull in literature on both sides of the paper's core claims. In the example run, three passes ran in parallel - proof verification, the theory-to-practice gap, and empirical design - and the proof specialist wrote enumeration scripts in `numpy` to test whether the theorem was true before judging its written proof.

Finally, the agent runs self-critiquing rounds before generating a report of findings grounded in cited evidence from the literature or code that disagrees with the paper's points. In the example run, this meant critique agents saw only the findings and the paper - not the specialists' reasoning - and tried to prove the authors right. The intuition behind separating critique from the main agent's context window is to de-bias the critique agents via a writer-reviewer pattern that Anthropic documents [here](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/best-practices).

The finding that survived to the top of the report aligns with the author's edits of their v1 paper:

> The proof asserts "full rank of Q" to conclude Q is a permutation matrix; this step is unjustified and, as written, circular... **The conclusion is nonetheless true**, but for the reason given in the next sentence, not the rank claim.

The authors' own v2, posted before this run, deleted that step and rewrote the proof along similar lines the report suggested.

`Deep Review` is built to be broadly useful across both quantitative and qualitative artifacts, but it excels on quantitative papers with a mathematical focus. Math and math-adjacent papers that contain concrete theorems that can be checked with code are a good place for users to start. I do, however, expect the tool to aid researchers significantly on open-ended problems as well.

### Why it helps users

For researchers, the skill lets users direct their attention to the claims that require the most editing, improving review velocity and reducing the time to publish.

For reviewers, it can reduce the time spent discovering preventable issues and increase the time spent on high-leverage questions around the contribution's novelty and what can make it better. I will caveat - lots of venues restrict feeding submissions to AI tools, so the reviewer-side benefit today comes largely from authors self-reviewing before submission. However, venues are increasingly willing to sanction AI-assisted review - see [AAAI-26 pilot](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13940). I expect this trend to continue since the cost of review does not scale linearly with submissions.

### Why review interfaces are essential

As AI systems get more powerful, products that improve human ability to review AI-generated work - what I call review interfaces - will get lots of usage. Even in coding with its verification advantages, humans are already a bottleneck - managing multiple Claude Code / Codex terminals requires mental strain and manual work. As personal AI scales, overseeing teams of agents that act on my behalf in the real world only widens the scope of the problem. Ultimately, I think there needs to be a wider set of review benchmarks that split into two tracks - both interesting for different reasons. The first measures the quality and correctness of AI reviews, run with and without a review harness like `Deep Review`. The ability for language models to scale performance on this benchmark could indicate the entire research loop can eventually be done autonomously. The second is capturing some measure of reduction of cognitive load imposed on a human by a review interface *across modalities* - voice, text, and image. In the same way that `Deep Review` attempts to provide expert review to the user via text, unlocking multi-modal review builds an ambient surface that users can interact with naturally - as two researchers would. Concretely, immediate future work could include benchmarking `Deep Review` systems against poorly reviewed papers on Open Review and increasing the pool of papers that the system checks in quantitative and qualitative domains.

### Appendix: Example Run

I wanted to choose an example that illustrates a real run of the skill that wasn't in-distribution already for the models. The latest OpenAI and Anthropic models - GPT 5.5, Opus 4.8 and Fable 5 - have a max training cutoff of January 2026, so I wanted to find a preprint from after that point to prevent contamination. Additionally, I wanted a preprint that had structural errors and *was subsequently corrected* to form a good test for the review tool.

I ran the tool on the following ML [paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22472) - submitted in May 2026 and amended on July 2. Find the attached report and v1 → v2 diff from the original paper [here](https://gist.github.com/dipakkrishnan/16e983d77c7267f06f25130c545ba83c). The review took ~18 minutes, fanned out the 3 specialist subagents and ran a self-critique round with 2 critique agents.

`Deep Review` found two critical flaws in the central proof: an unjustified "full rank" step used to conclude that the recovered code matrix is a permutation, and a non-sequitur invertibility claim connecting the optimal-predictor argument to the theorem. It also showed the theorem itself was still *true* by exhaustive enumeration in code - the gaps were in the written proof, not the result. The authors' v2 deleted both flagged steps and rewrote the proof along the same lines the report suggested, and fixed two typos the tool's report called out.

The run still contained a couple flaws. The paper's v2 also corrected a posterior formula and a row/column mistake that the review missed, and the report's empirical-design concerns went unaddressed in v2, so those remain open findings. Finally, I audited the full session transcript, including all 5 subagents for any web search contamination: zero web calls were made (all verification ran as local code) and there are zero references to v2 anywhere - so I have confidence the agent did not see the answer key.

A note on the example paper chosen - the paper was chosen because of the v1 → v2 characteristics and because it was published post training cutoff, in no way is this post a quality judgment. In fact, the report concluded the paper's result was correct. The intent is to show users how they can reduce volume of edits by using `Deep Review`. Of course, this is just one example, but I hope users can really push the tool for their own work!

### Appendix: Build Notes

Skills are an amazing distribution vehicle. I originally built out a bespoke UI that operationalized the user flow of uploading, answering questions and receiving an artifact + an agent loop built on the Claude Agent SDK. I eventually threw it out in favor of building a skill on top of coding agent harnesses that have all the batteries included. This commoditization of agent-building up the stack makes it simple to get something out to users as fast as possible.

Finally, it's also worth noting that there is an understated enablement gap today for non-software engineers that prevents them from using coding agents and learning how to get the most out of them. The terminal interface is a non-starter for lots of users. The Codex app is a great starting point for non-coding users to interact with agents, but really grokking how to get the most out of the tool has taken reps and is very non-obvious for non-technical users. I expect this problem to be fixed with some combination of product cleverness to embed the tools into areas users are already familiar with (Slack is one example, desktop apps, or even text?) and awareness / upskilling on what the coding agent harness represents and how to purpose it for a broad array of tasks - both in coding-adjacent domains and completely unrelated ones.
